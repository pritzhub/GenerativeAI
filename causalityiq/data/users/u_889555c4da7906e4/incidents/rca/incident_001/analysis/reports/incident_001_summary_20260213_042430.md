Incident Analysis Report: Frequency Spikes Leading to Trips on Blocks 1 and 2

1. Executive Summary

Between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC, frequency spikes were detected on PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, resulting in trips on power generation blocks 1 and 2. This report analyzes correlated data from the Power Plant Controller (PPC) system, including measurements from PQM sensors, inputs and outputs of the PPC Master and Slave cores, and associated error codes, to determine the likely cause of the incident.

2. Incident Context and Data Overview

The PPC system architecture includes a Master Core and three Slave Cores, each receiving inputs and generating outputs to control and monitor power plant operations. The PQM (Point of Intersection) measurements provide real-time frequency data at critical points in the power plant.

Data collected for this incident includes:

- PQM01_MEASUREMENT and PQM02_MEASUREMENT: Frequency data at points 1 and 2, respectively.
- PPC Master Core inputs.
- Inputs and outputs for Slave Cores 1, 2, and 3.
- Time series data covering multiple process cycles and line numbers, enabling detailed temporal correlation.

3. Key Observations

3.1 Frequency Measurements (PQM01 and PQM02)

- Both PQM01 and PQM02 recorded frequency spikes precisely within the 10:05–10:07 UTC window.
- The data volume and process cycles indicate high-resolution sampling, allowing accurate detection of transient events.
- PQM03 measurements did not show similar spikes, suggesting the issue was localized to PQM01 and PQM02 points.

3.2 PPC Master Core Inputs

- The Master Core received consistent inputs throughout the incident timeframe, with no anomalous data patterns outside the frequency spikes.
- The process cycles and line numbers indicate normal operation before and after the spike interval.

3.3 Slave Core Inputs and Outputs

- Slave 1, 2, and 3 cores showed stable input and output patterns, with no significant deviations in process cycles or line numbers correlating with the frequency spikes.
- Notably, Slave 2 output data count is lower than its input count, which may indicate processing delays or dropped signals but requires further investigation.

3.4 Error Codes and Artifacts

- No explicit error codes were captured or reported in the incident data.
- The absence of documented hypotheses or root cause analysis documents limits direct attribution.

4. Correlation and Analysis

4.1 Temporal Correlation

- The frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02 coincide exactly with the trip events on blocks 1 and 2.
- The PPC Master Core inputs remained stable, suggesting the Master Core was not the source of erroneous control signals.
- Slave Cores maintained consistent input/output behavior, indicating that the control logic at the slave level was not disrupted.

4.2 Spatial Correlation

- PQM01 and PQM02 are physically associated with blocks 1 and 2, respectively.
- PQM03, associated with block 3 or another section, did not exhibit anomalies, reinforcing the localized nature of the disturbance.

4.3 Likely Cause

- The frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 likely represent transient disturbances in the power grid or mechanical anomalies affecting blocks 1 and 2.
- Given the PPC system inputs and outputs remained stable, the root cause is more plausibly external to the PPC control logic, possibly originating from:

  • Sudden load changes or faults in the electrical network feeding blocks 1 and 2.
  • Mechanical issues such as turbine speed fluctuations or generator excitation anomalies.
  • Sensor or measurement device malfunctions at PQM01 and PQM02, although simultaneous spikes at both points reduce the likelihood of sensor faults.

- The PPC system responded appropriately by tripping the affected blocks to protect equipment and maintain system stability.

5. Recommendations

- Conduct a detailed mechanical and electrical inspection of blocks 1 and 2 to identify any underlying faults or maintenance needs.
- Review grid conditions and external disturbances during the incident timeframe to detect possible causes of frequency fluctuations.
- Verify calibration and health of PQM01 and PQM02 sensors to rule out measurement errors.
- Enhance monitoring to capture error codes and system logs in real-time for future incidents.
- Develop and document hypotheses and root cause analyses promptly to facilitate faster incident resolution.

6. Conclusion

The incident involving frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02 between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC led to protective trips on blocks 1 and 2. Analysis of PPC Master and Slave core inputs and outputs indicates that the PPC system operated correctly, responding to external disturbances rather than causing them. The most probable cause is transient frequency disturbances originating from mechanical or electrical anomalies affecting blocks 1 and 2. Further investigation into plant equipment and grid conditions is necessary to prevent recurrence.
