4. Correlation and Likely Cause

The frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02 coincide precisely with the timeframe during which the PPC Master and Slave cores were actively processing inputs and outputs. This temporal alignment suggests that the PPC control system was responding to or contributing to the observed frequency anomalies.

4.2 PPC Control Loop Dynamics

The PPC Master core orchestrates overall plant control, while Slave cores manage specific subsystems. The disparity in process cycles and data counts between inputs and outputs, especially in Slave 2, may indicate synchronization issues or delayed command execution.

4.3 Potential Root Cause

Given the frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 and the observed PPC core data patterns, the likely cause of the incident is a transient instability in the PPC control loops, possibly triggered by delayed or inconsistent input processing in Slave cores. This instability would manifest as frequency deviations detected by PQM sensors, leading to protective trips on blocks 1 and 2 to safeguard equipment.

5. Conclusion and Recommendations

The incident was primarily caused by frequency spikes detected at PQM01 and PQM02, which correlate with control input/output irregularities within the PPC Master and Slave cores. The data suggests that transient control loop instability, potentially due to delayed processing or communication issues in Slave cores, precipitated the trips on blocks 1 and 2.

To mitigate future occurrences, it is recommended to:

- Conduct a detailed review of Slave core processing performance and communication latency.
- Implement enhanced monitoring of PPC input/output synchronization.
- Review and optimize control loop parameters to improve stability under transient conditions.
- Ensure redundancy and fail-safe mechanisms are robust to handle transient frequency deviations without unnecessary trips.
