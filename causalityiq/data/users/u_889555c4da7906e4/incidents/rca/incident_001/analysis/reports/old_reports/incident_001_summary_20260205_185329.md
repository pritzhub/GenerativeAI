**Incident Analysis Report: Frequency Spikes Leading to Trips on Blocks 1 and 2**

---

### 1. Incident Overview

Between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC, frequency spikes were detected on PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, resulting in trips on power generation blocks 1 and 2. The Power Plant Controller (PPC) system, comprising a Master Core and multiple Slave Cores, is responsible for regulating and stabilizing power output. This report analyzes the correlation between PQM measurements, PPC Master and Slave Core inputs and outputs, and error codes to identify the likely root cause of the incident.

---

### 2. Data and Signal Summary

- **Signals Monitored:** 10 total, including:
  - PQM01_MEASUREMENT, PQM02_MEASUREMENT, PQM03_MEASUREMENT (frequency and power quality measurements at key points)
  - Inputs and outputs to/from Master Core PPC engine
  - Inputs and outputs to/from Slave 1, Slave 2, and Slave 3 Core PPC engines

- **Data Volume:**
  - PQM01 and PQM02 measurements: ~13,700 data points each
  - Master Core PPC input: 4,103 data points
  - Slave Core PPC inputs/outputs: ranges from ~1,494 to 2,407 data points depending on the slave

- **Artifacts:** 11 total (10 data files, 1 document), though the relevant RCA document was not found.

---

### 3. Key Observations from Time Series Data

- **Frequency Spikes on PQM01 and PQM02:**
  - Both PQM01 and PQM02 showed abnormal frequency spikes precisely within the 10:05–10:07 UTC window.
  - PQM03 measurements remained stable, indicating the issue was localized to PQM01 and PQM02 points.

- **Master Core PPC Input:**
  - The input data to the Master Core PPC engine showed consistent processing cycles and line numbers, indicating normal data flow.
  - No anomalies in input volume or timing were detected that could directly explain the frequency spikes.

- **Slave Core PPC Inputs and Outputs:**
  - Slave 1, Slave 2, and Slave 3 cores showed regular input and output patterns with no abrupt changes in data volume or processing cycles.
  - However, the output received from Slave 2 Core PPC engine showed fewer data points (1,503) compared to its input (2,407), suggesting possible data loss or processing delays.

- **Error Codes:**
  - No explicit error codes were captured or reported in the incident context.
  - The absence of error codes limits direct identification of hardware or software faults.

---

### 4. Correlation and Analysis

- The frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02 coincide with the timing of trips on blocks 1 and 2, indicating a causal relationship.

- PQM01 and PQM02 are measurement points likely associated with blocks 1 and 2 respectively, as their simultaneous disturbance triggered trips on these blocks.

- The Master Core PPC engine received stable inputs, suggesting that the issue did not originate from the Master Core’s data acquisition or processing.

- The Slave Core PPC engines, particularly Slave 2, showed a discrepancy between input and output data counts. This may indicate processing delays or communication issues within Slave 2, which could affect control signals sent to block 2.

- Since PQM03 remained stable and Slave 3 PPC data showed no anomalies, the problem appears isolated to the control and measurement chain involving blocks 1 and 2.

- The lack of error codes suggests the incident may be due to transient disturbances or subtle communication timing issues rather than outright hardware failure.

---

### 5. Likely Cause of the Problem

Based on the correlation of PQM measurements and PPC core data:

- The frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 were likely caused by transient disturbances in the control signals or feedback loops managed by the Slave PPC cores associated with blocks 1 and 2.

- The discrepancy in Slave 2 Core PPC input/output data suggests a potential delay or loss in control signal processing, which could have led to improper regulation of power output.

- This improper regulation would cause frequency instability at the measurement points, triggering protective trips on blocks 1 and 2 to prevent equipment damage.

- The Master Core PPC engine’s stable input data implies that the root cause lies downstream in the Slave PPC cores or in the communication channels between measurement points and Slave cores.

---

### 6. Recommendations

- **Detailed Communication Audit:** Investigate communication logs and timing between Master and Slave PPC cores, focusing on Slave 2, to identify delays or data loss.

- **Hardware and Software Diagnostics:** Perform diagnostics on Slave 1 and Slave 2 PPC hardware and software to detect latent faults or performance degradation.

- **Enhanced Monitoring:** Implement real-time monitoring and alerting on data discrepancies between Slave PPC inputs and outputs to preempt similar incidents.

- **Redundancy and Failover Testing:** Validate redundancy mechanisms for Slave PPC cores to ensure control continuity during transient disturbances.

- **Root Cause Documentation:** Locate or reconstruct the missing RCA document to capture detailed findings and corrective actions.

---

### 7. Conclusion

The incident involving frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02, resulting in trips on blocks 1 and 2, is most likely due to transient control signal disturbances originating within the Slave PPC cores, particularly Slave 2. These disturbances caused improper regulation of power output, triggering protective trips. Further investigation into Slave PPC communication and processing integrity is essential to prevent recurrence.
