Between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC, frequency spikes were detected on PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, which precipitated trips on power generation Blocks 1 and 2. 2. Incident Context and Data Overview  
The Power Plant Controller system comprises a Master core and three Slave cores, each receiving input signals and producing output signals that regulate plant operations. The incident data includes time series measurements from ten configured signals:  
- PQM01_MEASUREMENT, PQM02_MEASUREMENT, PQM03_MEASUREMENT (Point of Intersection measurements)  
- Inputs and outputs for the Master core and Slave cores 1, 2, and 3

- PQM01 and PQM02 measurements show frequency spikes during the incident window (10:05–10:07 UTC). These spikes are abnormal deviations from typical frequency values and are temporally aligned with the trips on Blocks 1 and 2.  
- The Master core PPC input data shows consistent processing cycles and line numbers, indicating stable data flow into the Master core before and after the incident window.  
- Slave cores 1, 2, and 3 inputs and outputs exhibit normal ranges in row numbers and process cycles, with no evident anomalies or irregularities coinciding with the frequency spikes.  
- PQM03 measurements remain stable, suggesting the issue was localized to PQM01 and PQM02 points.  
- No error codes or explicit fault messages were captured in the available data, limiting direct identification of software or hardware faults.

4. Correlation Analysis  
- The frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 are the earliest and most prominent anomalies detected. Given that these points correspond to critical measurement locations for Blocks 1 and 2, the spikes likely represent transient disturbances in the electrical network or sensor malfunctions.  
- The Master core PPC receives inputs from these PQM points and is responsible for coordinating control actions. The absence of irregularities in the Master core input data suggests that the PPC engine processed the frequency spikes as valid inputs, triggering protective trips to safeguard equipment.  
- Slave cores did not exhibit input or output anomalies, indicating that the control logic and communication channels within the PPC system were operational and not the source of the disturbance.  
- The trips on Blocks 1 and 2 are consistent with protective responses to frequency excursions detected at PQM01 and PQM02, confirming that the PPC system acted as designed in response to abnormal frequency conditions.

5. Likely Cause of the Problem  
Based on the correlation of PQM measurements and PPC core inputs/outputs, the most probable cause of the incident is a transient frequency disturbance localized at PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points. Possible underlying factors include:  
- External grid disturbances or load fluctuations causing rapid frequency changes at these points.  
- Sensor or measurement device anomalies producing erroneous frequency readings, leading to false triggering of protection mechanisms.  
- Electrical faults or transient events in the network segment monitored by PQM01 and PQM02.

Given the stable operation of the PPC Master and Slave cores and the absence of error codes, internal controller malfunction is unlikely. The PPC system correctly interpreted the frequency spikes and initiated trips to prevent damage.

6. Recommendations  
- Conduct detailed inspection and calibration of PQM01 and PQM02 measurement devices to rule out sensor faults.  
- Review grid operational logs and external event records for disturbances coinciding with the incident time to identify external causes.  
- Implement enhanced filtering or validation logic in the PPC system to differentiate between transient measurement noise and genuine frequency excursions, reducing unnecessary trips.  
- Establish continuous monitoring and alerting for frequency anomalies at critical PQM points to enable proactive response.

7. Conclusion  
The incident involving frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 led to protective trips on Blocks 1 and 2. Analysis indicates that the PPC system functioned correctly in response to abnormal frequency inputs, with no evidence of internal controller faults. The root cause is likely external frequency disturbances or sensor anomalies at the affected measurement points. Further investigation and preventive measures are advised to mitigate recurrence.
