Incident Analysis Report: Frequency Spikes and Subsequent Trips on Power Plant Blocks 1 and 2

1. Executive Summary  
Between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC, frequency spikes were observed on PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, which directly resulted in trips on power plant blocks 1 and 2. This report analyzes the correlation between the PQM measurements, inputs and outputs of the Power Plant Controller (PPC) Master and Slave cores, and available error codes to identify the likely root cause of the incident.

2. Incident Context and Data Overview  
The incident involved multiple signals monitored across the PPC system, including:  
- PQM01_MEASUREMENT and PQM02_MEASUREMENT, representing frequency measurements at key points of intersection (PQM points 1 and 2).  
- Inputs and outputs to/from the PPC Master core and three Slave cores, which coordinate control and protection functions for the power plant blocks.  

Data collected includes time series measurements with thousands of data points, covering process cycles and line numbers, enabling detailed temporal correlation analysis.

3. Key Observations from Time Series Data  
- PQM01 and PQM02 measurements showed significant frequency spikes precisely during the 10:05–10:07 UTC window. These spikes are abnormal deviations from nominal frequency values and are critical indicators of system instability.  
- Inputs to the PPC Master core and Slave cores were consistent in volume and timing, indicating that control commands were being issued regularly. However, outputs from Slave cores, particularly Slave 1 and Slave 2, showed slight discrepancies in count and timing compared to inputs, suggesting potential processing delays or errors.  
- The process cycles for Slave cores averaged around 150 cycles, while the Master core operated at a higher mean process cycle count (~228), consistent with its supervisory role.  
- No explicit error codes or fault messages were captured in the available data or documentation, limiting direct identification of hardware or software faults.

4. Correlation and Causal Analysis  
- The frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 are the primary triggers for the trips on blocks 1 and 2. These PQM points are critical measurement nodes feeding real-time frequency data to the PPC system.  
- The PPC Master core receives these measurements as inputs and coordinates with Slave cores to execute control actions. The observed timing and volume of inputs and outputs suggest that the Master core processed the frequency anomalies and issued trip commands accordingly.  
- The slight mismatch in input-output counts for Slave cores, especially Slave 1 and Slave 2, may indicate transient communication delays or processing bottlenecks during the spike event. Such delays could exacerbate the system’s response time, contributing to protective trips.  
- Given the absence of error codes, the most plausible cause is an external disturbance or transient event affecting the frequency at PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, rather than an internal PPC hardware or software failure. This disturbance triggered protective logic within the PPC system, resulting in trips to safeguard the power plant blocks.

5. Likely Root Cause  
The incident was most likely caused by a transient frequency disturbance detected at PQM01 and PQM02. This disturbance could have originated from grid instability, sudden load changes, or faults external to the PPC system. The PPC Master and Slave cores responded appropriately by initiating trips on blocks 1 and 2 to protect equipment and maintain system integrity. Minor discrepancies in Slave core processing suggest possible communication latency but are not primary causes.

6. Recommendations  
- Investigate external grid conditions and events coinciding with the 10:05–10:07 UTC window to identify the source of frequency disturbances.  
- Review communication protocols and processing performance between PPC Master and Slave cores to minimize latency and ensure timely response during transient events.  
- Enhance monitoring and logging to capture error codes or fault messages for future incidents to facilitate faster root cause identification.  
- Consider implementing additional filtering or adaptive control algorithms in PPC to better handle transient frequency spikes without unnecessary trips, where safe and feasible.

7. Conclusion  
The frequency spikes at PQM01 and PQM02 were the initiating factors for the trips on power plant blocks 1 and 2. The PPC system’s Master and Slave cores functioned as designed by detecting the anomalies and executing protective trips. The incident underscores the importance of robust frequency measurement and rapid control coordination to maintain power plant stability. Further investigation into external grid events and PPC communication performance is advised to prevent recurrence.
