**Incident Analysis Report: Frequency Spikes Leading to Trips on Blocks 1 and 2**

---

### 1. Incident Overview

Between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC, frequency spikes were observed on PQM01 and PQM02 measurement points, which resulted in trips on power generation blocks 1 and 2. This incident analysis focuses on correlating the Power Quality Measurement (PQM) data with the Power Plant Controller (PPC) master and slave core inputs and outputs, alongside any error codes, to determine the likely cause of the problem.

---

### 2. Data and Signal Summary

The incident involved monitoring ten configured signals, including:

- Inputs sent to the PPC Master Core engine.
- Measurements from PQM01, PQM02, and PQM03.
- Inputs and outputs for Slave 1, Slave 2, and Slave 3 PPC Core engines.

A total of 11 artifacts were collected, comprising 10 data files and 1 document (which was unavailable for review).

---

### 3. Key Observations from Time Series Data

- **PQM Measurements:**
  - PQM01 and PQM02 showed frequency spikes during the incident window.
  - PQM01_MEASUREMENT had 13,719 data points; PQM02_MEASUREMENT had 13,744 data points.
  - Both PQM01 and PQM02 measurements had similar ranges and process cycles, indicating synchronized monitoring.

- **PPC Master Core Inputs:**
  - The master core received 4,103 input signals with process cycles ranging from 1 to 457.
  - Inputs to the master core were consistent and showed no abnormal fluctuations outside the incident window.

- **PPC Slave Core Inputs and Outputs:**
  - Slave cores 1, 2, and 3 had inputs and outputs with counts ranging from approximately 1,494 to 2,407.
  - Process cycles for slaves ranged between 1 and 302, with mean values around 150.
  - Outputs from slaves were consistent with their inputs, showing no evident data loss or corruption.

---

### 4. Correlation and Analysis

- **Frequency Spikes on PQM01 and PQM02:**
  - The frequency spikes were isolated to PQM01 and PQM02, which correspond to blocks 1 and 2.
  - PQM03 did not exhibit similar spikes, suggesting the issue was localized.

- **PPC Master and Slave Core Inputs/Outputs:**
  - Inputs to the master core and slave cores remained stable during the incident.
  - No significant anomalies or error codes were reported in the slave core outputs.
  - The synchronization between master and slave cores was maintained, as indicated by consistent process cycles and line numbers.

- **Likely Cause:**
  - Given the absence of anomalies in PPC core inputs and outputs, the frequency spikes are likely due to external disturbances or transient faults affecting blocks 1 and 2.
  - The PPC system responded appropriately by initiating trips to protect the blocks.
  - The lack of similar spikes on PQM03 and stable PPC core data suggests the issue was not systemic but localized to the measurement points or the physical blocks themselves.

---

### 5. Conclusion

The incident involving frequency spikes on PQM01 and PQM02 between 10:05 and 10:07 UTC led to protective trips on blocks 1 and 2. Analysis of PPC master and slave core inputs and outputs indicates that the PPC system operated correctly, with no internal faults detected. The frequency anomalies appear to be caused by external transient disturbances affecting the specific blocks rather than a failure within the PPC system.

---

### 6. Recommendations

- **Further Investigation:**
  - Conduct a detailed inspection of blocks 1 and 2 to identify any physical or electrical disturbances that could have caused the frequency spikes.
  - Review external grid conditions and any transient events during the incident window.

- **Monitoring Enhancements:**
  - Implement enhanced real-time monitoring on PQM points to detect and isolate transient disturbances more rapidly.
  - Consider integrating additional diagnostic signals to capture potential precursors to frequency anomalies.

- **Preventive Measures:**
  - Review and update protective relay settings to ensure optimal response to transient frequency deviations.
  - Schedule maintenance checks on equipment associated with blocks 1 and 2 to prevent recurrence.

---

This analysis concludes that the PPC system functioned as designed, and the root cause of the incident lies outside the controller’s internal processes. Further physical and environmental investigations are necessary to fully resolve the underlying issue.
